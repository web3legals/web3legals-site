India's Crypto Legal Framework in 2026: What Every Web3 Founder Must Know

By Rahul Pareek · 2026-06-21T11:53 · 8 min read

Introduction

India is home to one of the world's largest crypto user bases — over 100 million people have interacted with digital assets. Yet for years, the legal landscape remained a patchwork of contradictory signals: a Supreme Court that struck down the RBI banking ban, a government that considered a blanket prohibition, and regulators that issued warnings without clear frameworks.

In 2026, that ambiguity is largely over. India now has a functioning — if still evolving — crypto regulatory framework. And every Web3 founder building in India, targeting Indian users, or raising funds from Indian investors needs to understand it in full.

This is not a guide to avoiding regulation. It is a guide to building a sustainable, compliant Web3 business in the world's most important emerging crypto market.

*

1. The Foundation: How India Regulates Crypto Assets

India does not have a single comprehensive "Crypto Law." Instead, regulation flows through four overlapping frameworks:

The Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) — 2023 Amendment

The most significant regulatory development was the March 2023 amendment bringing Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) under the PMLA. This means:

  • All entities dealing in VDAs are now Reporting Entities under PMLA
  • Mandatory KYC/AML obligations apply — customer identification, transaction monitoring, suspicious transaction reporting
  • All VDA businesses must register with the Financial Intelligence Unit — India (FIU-IND)
  • Records must be maintained for 5 years
  • Violations carry penalties of up to ₹1 crore per instance plus potential criminal prosecution
What this means for founders: If your platform facilitates buying, selling, exchanging, or transferring VDAs — even in a DeFi context — you likely have PMLA obligations. "We are decentralized" is not a recognized exemption under Indian law.

The Income Tax Framework — Section 115BBH

Since April 2022, India has imposed a flat 30% tax on profits from VDA transfers, with no deductions allowed except the cost of acquisition. Additionally, a 1% TDS applies on VDA transfers above ₹10,000 per year (₹50,000 for specified persons).

For Web3 businesses, this creates operational challenges:

  • Token distributions, airdrops, and staking rewards may constitute taxable income
  • NFT sales are taxable as VDA transfers
  • Employee token compensation triggers tax obligations

SEBI's Expanding Role

The Securities and Exchange Board of India has been progressively asserting jurisdiction over crypto assets that exhibit characteristics of securities. In 2024-25, SEBI issued guidance indicating that:

  • Tokens that function as investment contracts fall under SEBI's regulatory perimeter
  • Crypto investment platforms may require SEBI registration as Investment Advisers or Portfolio Managers
  • DeFi protocols facilitating leveraged trading may need exchange recognition

The Digital Asset Bill (Anticipated)

India's long-anticipated comprehensive crypto legislation is expected to provide a unified regulatory framework covering:

  • Classification of digital assets (utility, security, payment, commodity)
  • Licensing requirements for VDA service providers
  • Consumer protection obligations
  • Cross-border transaction rules
*

2. FIU-IND Registration: What It Is and Who Needs It

The Financial Intelligence Unit — India (FIU-IND) is the central agency responsible for receiving, processing, and analyzing financial intelligence related to money laundering and terrorist financing.

Who Must Register

Under the PMLA 2023 amendment, the following VDA business types must register with FIU-IND:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and increasingly decentralized)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • VDA transfer services
  • Platforms facilitating NFT trading above threshold values
  • Crypto payment processors

The Registration Process

  1. Create an account on the FIU-IND reporting portal (fiuindia.gov.in)
  2. Submit entity details, beneficial ownership information, and compliance officer appointment
  3. Implement required AML/CFT policies and procedures
  4. Begin filing Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) and Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) as applicable

Non-registration carries penalties and reputational risk, including potential banking relationship termination.

*

3. Token Classification Under Indian Law

India has adopted the term "Virtual Digital Asset" (VDA) as the umbrella classification under the Income Tax Act. However, for regulatory purposes, the classification question is more nuanced.

The Three-Category Framework

Category 1: Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) The broadest category — includes Bitcoin, Ethereum, and most fungible tokens. Subject to 30% tax and PMLA obligations. Category 2: Securities Tokens that meet the characteristics of securities under the SEBI Act — offering profit expectations based on the issuer's efforts. These require compliance with SEBI's framework, including potential registration as a public offering. Category 3: Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs) Generally classified as VDAs for tax purposes, but the regulatory treatment of NFTs representing real-world assets or financial instruments is still evolving. *

4. Cross-Border Transactions and FEMA

India's Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) regulates cross-border money flows — and crypto is no exception.

Key points for Web3 founders:

  • Receiving foreign investment in a crypto project must comply with FEMA's Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) rules
  • Indian residents investing abroad in crypto projects may have reporting obligations under the Liberalized Remittance Scheme (LRS)
  • Token sales to foreign investors from an Indian entity require careful structuring under FEMA

For most Web3 startups raising international capital, the practical solution is to incorporate a holding entity outside India (Singapore, UAE, or Cayman are most common) while maintaining the operational team in India.

*

5. Practical Compliance Checklist for Indian Web3 Founders

Immediate Actions (Before Launch)

  • Assess your token against VDA classification, securities law, and PMLA obligations
  • Register with FIU-IND if your platform facilitates VDA transactions
  • Implement a KYC/AML program — customer identification, transaction monitoring, STR filing procedures
  • Ensure your Terms of Service and Privacy Policy address Indian user rights under the DPDP Act 2023
  • Consult a qualified Web3 lawyer on your entity structure

Ongoing Compliance

  • File required reports with FIU-IND (STRs, CTRs)
  • Maintain 5-year records of all VDA transactions and customer information
  • Deduct and deposit 1% TDS on qualifying VDA transfers
  • Monitor SEBI guidance for any new obligations affecting your platform
  • Review compliance posture annually as the regulatory framework evolves
*

6. The Banking Problem — And How to Navigate It

Indian banks remain cautious about crypto-related business accounts. Despite the Supreme Court striking down the RBI banking ban in 2020, many banks informally refuse accounts to crypto businesses or restrict transfers.

Practical strategies for Indian Web3 founders:

  • Use multiple banking relationships — don't rely on a single bank
  • Incorporate internationally for your primary treasury if operating globally
  • Work with fintech-friendly banks — some smaller private banks and payment banks are more open
  • Use compliant crypto-to-fiat rails — licensed payment aggregators that support VDA businesses
*

7. Conclusion

India's crypto legal framework in 2026 is complex — but it is navigable. The founders who treat compliance as a foundation will build the most durable Web3 businesses in the world's most important emerging crypto market.
